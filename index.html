Tea, Labor, and the Long Shadow of the Plantation

An Economic Analysis of Assam Tea, Labor, and Global Markets.

Author Aaira Goswami Affiliation Stanford University

Project overview

Assam tea, labor markets, and the distribution of value

This capstone website organizes evidence on India’s organized tea plantation sector with emphasis on Assam, where independent investigations and peer-reviewed work document acute stress on wages, health, housing, and voice—especially for women workers. The reading list combines a major NGO briefing (Oxfam, 2019), a sociological health review (Saikia & Parida, 2026), investigative journalism synthesizing historians’ work (Indian Express, 2025), a linguistic essay on migration and culture (Avtans, 2025), governance commentary on IFC-linked plantations (Sachdev & Satpute / ETI), and primary legal entry points (Tea Board PLA PDF; Indian Kanoon listing—verify text manually).

The site separates (i) verbatim or closely paraphrased claims tied to named documents from (ii) illustrative charts that remain pedagogical until you replace them with your own harmonized datasets.

Scope note: Oxfam’s briefing reports household-level patterns from its field visits; it is advocacy research and should be read alongside government statistics and peer-reviewed studies, not as a substitute for them.

Research question

How do value-chain price distribution, statutory labor and health protections, and documented governance gaps jointly relate to labor and health outcomes for women workers in Assam’s organized tea sector?

Components map to Oxfam (consumer-price shares), the Tea Plantation Act of 1951 as discussed in Saikia & Parida (2026), and CAO/IFC findings summarized in Sachdev & Satpute (ETI blog)—see extraction file for boundaries.

Historical foundations

Colonial institutions and gendered plucking (as reported in secondary sources)

Indian Express (2025) synthesizes historians’ accounts of penal contracts after the Workmen’s Breach of Contract Act (1859), recruitment of women and children, and wage discounts justified by planters as “lighter” work—attributing specific statistics and quotations to Jayeeta Sharma, Samita Sen, Srijita Chakravarty, Andrew B. Liu, Rana Pratap Behal, and others. This section does not substitute for reading those primary scholarly works; it records only that the newspaper article presents those claims and interviews.

Supported detail (via journalism only): The article reports Liu’s statement that women were about half the nineteenth-century workforce on these lines and were paid about 20% less than men with a rationale of lighter work.

Indian Express (2025). For linguistics of migration, see Avtans (2025) card below.

Law, health, and contemporary structure

From statute to outcomes

Saikia & Parida (2026), in a literature review, ask why reported diseases among female laborers in Assam’s tea gardens have risen despite assurances of adequate health care under the Tea Plantation Act of 1951, and frame management pressure on “female labour” as shaping sanitation, hygiene, and health. Separately, Sachdev & Satpute (ETI blog) discuss Indian labour law compliance, wage levels relative to legal minima as they report them, and CAO findings on freedom of association and grievance mechanisms for IFC-linked plantations.

Primary law PDF (not summarized here): Tea Board India hosts an amended Plantations Labour Act PDF — open and cite specific sections after reading: Plantations Labour Act (PDF). Indian Kanoon document hub: indiankanoon.org/doc/703097 (automated fetch blocked; verify title in browser).

Briefing paper

Addressing the Human Cost of Assam Tea

Banerji, S., & Willoughby, R. (2019). Oxfam. DOI 10.21201/2019.4870

RQ: Why are living wages and decent conditions systematically denied, and what reforms (price sharing, gender policy, law review) does Oxfam advance?

Method: Oxfam-commissioned household visits (stated on resource page).

Findings (overview): 50% of visited households held Assam BPL ration cards; healthcare/water/education struggles; brands/supermarkets may capture up to ~95% of end consumer price vs <~5% remaining on estates in some cases; women in lowest-paid plucking and unpaid care.

Why it matters: Explicit value-chain rent distribution narrative for your economics frame.

Peer-reviewed review

Brewing Inequality…

Saikia, V., & Parida, P. K. (2026). Int J Soc Determinants Health Health Serv, 56(2), 256–261. DOI 10.1177/27551938251366878

RQ (abstract): How do social disparities among female tea workers relate to health disparities, and why rising reported disease despite Tea Plantation Act 1951 assurances?

Method: Extensive literature review (PubMed publication type: Review).

Findings (abstract only): Underpayment and marginalisation of women; coercion of “female labour” linked to sanitation, hygiene, and health; calls for comprehensive policies.

Why it matters: Bridges labour economics and health inequality with statutory reference.

Essay / Himal

The languages of tea-estate workers in Assam and Darjeeling

Avtans, A. (2025, Jan 20). Himal Southasian

RQ (dek): How did migration to colonial tea gardens reshape language contact, lingua francas, and mother tongues?

Method: Not stated in retrieved page fragment (essay form).

Findings (visible lede): Diverse marginalised migrants brought eclectic languages; new lingua francas; erosion of many mother tongues.

Why it matters: Adds cultural / linguistic capital dimension alongside wage and health evidence.

Governance / blog

A life of dignity long overdue for Assam’s tea workers

Sachdev, S., & Satpute, J. (guest). Ethical Trading Initiative

RQ: Did IFC/Tata APPL deliver promised conditions and worker voice?

Method: Commentary referencing CAO report, IFC investment figures, complainant NGOs.

Findings: IFC US $7.8m for 19.9% stake; CAO Nov 2016 report; blog lists CAO headline findings (wages, housing, health, child labour risk, freedom of association, pesticides, Indigenous classification, etc.); states Rs 126/day vs Rs 250 legal minimum at time of writing; argues boycotts harmful.

Why it matters: Development finance + certification limits + FOA for policy economics.

Methodology

Evidence synthesis (what this site does and does not do)

What is done here: Each reading-list item is parsed for bibliographic metadata, stated research questions, declared methods (where authors declare them), and findings that appear in abstracts, overviews, or blog text. Claims are logged in docs/READING_LIST_EXTRACTION.md with explicit boundaries (e.g., journalism vs peer review; PDFs not machine-read).

What is not done here: No new econometric estimates, causal identification, or merged micro-datasets are produced on this page. Any chart labelled “illustrative” is pedagogical until you substitute coefficients or indices from your own analysis. For your capstone empirical strategy, you might next: (1) download official wage and production series; (2) code policy events from the Plantations Labour Act PDF and amendments; (3) replicate or extend review themes from Saikia & Parida using primary health data—those steps are your design choices once data access is confirmed.

Policy & economic implications

Implications supported by the reading list (not an exhaustive policy menu)

Oxfam (2019) explicitly points to fairer sharing of end consumer price, stronger gender policies, and review of plantation labour laws—conditional on its diagnosis of poverty, ration-card reliance, and care burdens. Saikia & Parida (2026) argue for comprehensive policies improving well-being given reported health burdens among women workers (abstract-level claim). Sachdev & Satpute (ETI) argue World Bank / IFC action plans were too narrow, that Indian labour-law compliance must improve, that certification schemes cannot be relied on alone, and that boycotting Assam tea would harm workers—advocating consumer pressure on IFC and APPL instead.

Legal text: Operational implications of the Plantations Labour Act require line-by-line reading of the Tea Board PDF and any case compilation you trust (e.g. Indian Kanoon after manual access)—this site does not interpret sections not yet excerpted.

Conclusion

The reading list converges on a small set of economic themes: how consumer-price shares and reported estate wages relate to statutory minima and in-kind obligations, how health outcomes are discussed relative to plantation labour law, and how governance actors (states, firms, IFC, unions) interact on enforcement and freedom of association.

This website does not adjudicate which single narrative is “correct”; it shows that Oxfam, peer-reviewed sociology of health, historians via journalism, and CAO-related commentary each supply partial, checkable pieces of a larger empirical puzzle your capstone can assemble with additional administrative and survey data.

Next step for a publishable thesis: download the Tea Board PLA PDF and official wage series; reconcile ETI wage figures with current notifications; and replace illustrative charts with indices whose numerators and denominators you document.
